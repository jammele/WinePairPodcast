If you love Malbec, you already know what you're about: dark fruit, a smooth mouthfeel, enough tannin to feel real but not enough to ruin the second glass. Argentina's flagship red sells well for a reason. It delivers at $15–25 what a lot of pricier wines only promise.

The good news is that a lot of other wines scratch the same itch. Some are bolder, some softer, some trade the blueberry for black pepper or smoke. All of them are worth knowing.

Here are six wines to try if Malbec is where you live.

## The place to start: Malbec (Mendoza, Argentina)

**Bold and Fruit-Forward**

[INSERT CARD: Malbec Anchor]

Malbec from Mendoza is the textbook bold red for anyone who prefers dark fruit to earth, smooth tannins to grip, and everyday pleasure to occasion-wine complexity. Argentina rescued it from obscurity — the grape is technically one of Bordeaux's six permitted blending varieties, but it never found a starring role in France. In Mendoza's high-altitude vineyards, it found its thing: blueberry and plum in the foreground, chocolate and coffee underneath, tannins that are firm but never aggressive, and just enough oak to add structure without tasting like a furniture store.

It doesn't need food. It just works. That's the appeal, and it's the north star for everything below.

## Six wines to try next

The wines below all share what makes Malbec work: dark fruit, enough body to feel substantial, and a mouthfeel that doesn't make you wince. What changes is the grape, the country, and sometimes the direction the boldness takes. Some are smokier, some are jammier, some are more complex. All are worth a bottle.

## Syrah / Shiraz

**Rich and Savory**

[INSERT CARD: Syrah/Shiraz]

**What you'll recognize from Malbec:** Dark fruit in the same neighborhood — blackberry, plum, black cherry. Similar body and tannin weight. The structure lands in a comparable place.

**What's different:** Syrah is savory where Malbec is fruity. You'll find black pepper, smoked meat, and sometimes olive where Malbec gives you blueberry and chocolate. French Syrah from the Northern Rhône leans hard into smoke and violet. Australian Shiraz swings back toward fruit-forward with higher alcohol and more warmth. Same grape, different personality depending on where it grew up.

**Try this if:** You've been drinking Malbec long enough to want something with more going on. Syrah is the natural step up for anyone who wants bold and dark without giving up complexity.

## Zinfandel

**Bold and Fruit-Forward**

[INSERT CARD: Zinfandel]

**What you'll recognize from Malbec:** Bold, dark fruit, and filling. The same "this wine doesn't need a meal" energy. Medium-high tannin with a warm finish.

**What's different:** Zinfandel is jammier — blackberry jam, not fresh blackberry. Higher alcohol, almost always. There's a spice note running through it (cinnamon, black pepper, mocha) that Malbec doesn't carry. Quality varies a lot by producer; the best California Zins are genuinely worth hunting down.

**Try this if:** You want a bolder, more American version of the bold red profile. It's especially good with BBQ — ribs, pulled pork, anything with a sticky sauce.

## Grenache

**Rich and Savory**

[INSERT CARD: Grenache]

**What you'll recognize from Malbec:** Fruit-forward and warm, with enough body to feel substantial. Similar lower acidity. You'll still get dark fruit and a smooth finish.

**What's different:** Grenache is softer. Less tannin, lighter body, more strawberry and red fruit than the deep black fruit of Malbec. Spanish Garnacha is the same grape — often excellent value. It's a friendlier, more approachable version of the bold-and-fruity category, and it has more versatility with food.

**Try this if:** You like Malbec's fruit and drinkability but want something a bit lighter, or something that works better at the dinner table. Grenache is the most food-flexible wine on this list.

## Nero d'Avola

**Bold and Fruit-Forward**

[INSERT CARD: Nero d'Avola]

**What you'll recognize from Malbec:** Similar dark fruit character — blackberry, plum — with similar body and warmth.

**What's different:** Nero d'Avola is Sicilian, and it shows. There's a spice and herbal note that Malbec doesn't carry. Less oak as a rule. The tannins are there but the structure is a bit more angular — it's not quite as smooth straight out of the bottle as a good Mendoza Malbec.

**Try this if:** You want a value pick with dark fruit that has some regional character. Good entry point for anyone curious about Italian red wine who isn't ready to deal with Barolo's learning curve.

## Pinotage

**Bold and Fruit-Forward**

[INSERT CARD: Pinotage]

**What you'll recognize from Malbec:** Dark fruit and a bold body with similar tannin structure. It drinks like a serious red.

**What's different:** Pinotage has a signature smokiness — like the grape arrived pre-seasoned for the grill. It's South Africa's own grape, a cross between Pinot Noir and Cinsault, and it carries a slightly earthy, funky character that splits opinion. Some people love it immediately. Some people need a bottle to warm up to it. The smoke is real and intentional.

**Try this if:** You want something adventurous with Malbec's weight but its own distinct personality. It's especially at home with grilled meat.

## Primitivo

**Bold and Fruit-Forward**

[INSERT CARD: Primitivo]

**What you'll recognize from Malbec:** Almost everything. Same bold, fruit-forward style, similar tannin, similar warmth. This is the closest thing to a direct swap on the list.

**What's different:** Primitivo is genetically identical to Zinfandel and comes from Puglia in southern Italy. Where Malbec runs toward blueberry and chocolate, Primitivo leans into dried fig, blackberry jam, and black pepper. Higher alcohol and a richer finish. Italian character where Malbec has Argentine character.

**Try this if:** You want something extremely close to Malbec in weight and style but with a different address on the label. It's also often the best value on this list — solid bottles under $15 are common.

## Food pairings that work across the group

Malbec's profile — dark fruit, medium-high tannin, lower acidity — makes it a natural match with grilled and roasted red meat. The tannin grips fat, the fruit plays off charred edges, and the smoky side of a good Malbec mirrors the smoke from the grill. That logic transfers to every wine on this list.

Where Grenache and Nero d'Avola offer more acidity, you get more flexibility at the table. They'll hold up alongside pasta and lighter dishes where a full Malbec might overpower.

Pairings that work for all six:
- Steak — grilled, roasted, or braised
- BBQ — ribs, brisket, pulled pork
- Lamb chops or leg of lamb
- Empanadas, especially beef
- Hard aged cheese — manchego, aged cheddar, pecorino
- Mushroom-heavy dishes — risotto, pasta, grain bowls

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What wine is similar to Malbec but lighter?**
Grenache. It keeps the fruit-forward, low-acidity profile that makes Malbec easy to drink but with less tannin and a bit more lift. Spanish Garnacha is the same grape, often cheaper, and works everywhere Malbec does. If you want Malbec's approachability without quite as much weight, Grenache is the right answer.

**Is Malbec similar to Cabernet Sauvignon?**
They're in the same family — both bold reds with dark fruit and tannin — but Cabernet is significantly firmer and more structured. Malbec is smoother and more immediately approachable. If you find Cab too grippy, Malbec is the friendlier version. They're also related: Malbec is actually one of the six grapes permitted in Bordeaux blends alongside Cabernet.

**What's the difference between Malbec and Syrah?**
Malbec is fruitier and smoother; Syrah is savory and more complex. Both are bold and dark-fruited, but Syrah adds black pepper, smoked meat, and sometimes olive. If you've been drinking Malbec for a while and want something that rewards more attention, Syrah is the natural next step.

**Are there good Malbec alternatives under $15?**
Yes — Primitivo from Puglia is genetically the same grape as Zinfandel and drinks like a richer Malbec cousin. Solid bottles regularly come in under $15. Nero d'Avola from Sicily is another strong dark-fruit pick in that range.

**Can you drink Malbec with fish?**
You can, but it's not the best match. The tannin and dark fruit tend to overwhelm delicate seafood. If you want a red with fish, Grenache is the better call — lighter, more acidic, less likely to fight the food. If it's grilled swordfish or salmon, you might get away with Malbec, but Grenache handles it more cleanly.
