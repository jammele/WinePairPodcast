If you love Malbec, you already know what you're about: dark fruit, a smooth mouthfeel, enough tannin to feel real but not enough to ruin the second glass. Argentina's flagship red sells well for a reason. It delivers at $15 to $25 what a lot of pricier wines only promise.

## The place to start: Malbec (Mendoza, Argentina)

[INSERT CARD: Malbec Anchor]

Malbec from Mendoza is the textbook bold red for anyone who prefers dark fruit to earth, smooth tannins to grip, and everyday pleasure to occasion-wine complexity. Argentina rescued it from obscurity. The grape is technically one of Bordeaux's six permitted blending varieties, but it never found a starring role in France. In Mendoza's high-altitude vineyards, it found its thing: blueberry and plum in the foreground, chocolate and coffee underneath, tannins that are firm but never aggressive, and just enough oak to add structure without tasting like a furniture store.

It doesn't need food. It just works. That's the appeal, and it's the north star for everything below.

## Six wines to try next

The wines below all share what makes Malbec work: dark fruit, enough body to feel substantial, and a mouthfeel that doesn't make you wince. What changes is the grape, the country, and sometimes the direction the boldness takes. Some are spicier, some are jammier, some are more complex. All are worth a bottle.

## Syrah / Shiraz

[INSERT CARD: Syrah/Shiraz]

**What you'll recognize from Malbec:** Dark fruit, similar body, and enough tannin to feel substantial. You're still in bold red territory.

**What's different:** Syrah is spicy in a way Malbec isn't. Black pepper is the signature note, and it runs through every glass. You'll also get dark fruit and a smoky, meaty quality that Malbec doesn't carry. Northern Rhone Syrah from France leans into the pepper and violet hard. Australian Shiraz swings back toward fruit-forward with more alcohol and warmth. Same grape, completely different mood depending on where it grew up.

**Try this if:** You've been drinking Malbec for a while and want something that rewards more attention. Syrah is the natural step up.

## Zinfandel

[INSERT CARD: Zinfandel]

**What you'll recognize from Malbec:** Bold, dark fruit, and filling. The same "this wine doesn't need a meal" energy. Medium-high tannin with a warm finish.

**What's different:** Zinfandel is jammier. Blackberry jam, not fresh blackberry. Higher alcohol, almost always. There's a spice note running through it, cinnamon and mocha, that Malbec doesn't carry. Quality varies a lot by producer. The best California Zins are worth hunting down.

**Try this if:** You want a bolder, more American version of the bold red profile. Especially good with BBQ, ribs, or pulled pork.

## Grenache

[INSERT CARD: Grenache]

**What you'll recognize from Malbec:** Fruit-forward and warm, with enough body to feel substantial. Similar lower acidity. Still dark and drinkable.

**What's different:** Grenache is softer. Less tannin, lighter body, more strawberry and red fruit than the deep black fruit of Malbec. Spanish Garnacha is the same grape, often excellent value. It's the friendlier, more approachable path through bold-and-fruity territory.

**Try this if:** You like Malbec's approachability but want something a bit lighter, or something that works better at the table. Grenache is the most food-flexible wine on this list.

## Nero d'Avola

[INSERT CARD: Nero d'Avola]

**What you'll recognize from Malbec:** Similar dark fruit, similar body, similar warmth. You're still in blackberry-and-plum country.

**What's different:** Nero d'Avola is Sicilian, and it shows. There's a spice and herbal note that Malbec doesn't carry. Less oak as a rule. The tannins are there but more angular. It's not as immediately smooth as a good Mendoza Malbec straight out of the bottle.

**Try this if:** You want a value pick with dark fruit that has some regional character. A good entry point for anyone curious about Italian reds who isn't ready to deal with Barolo's learning curve.

## Pinotage

[INSERT CARD: Pinotage]

**What you'll recognize from Malbec:** Dark fruit and a bold body with similar tannin structure. It drinks like a serious red.

**What's different:** Pinotage has a signature smokiness. It comes from South Africa and carries an earthy, smoky character that splits opinion. Some people love it immediately. Some people need a bottle to warm up to it. The smoke is real and intentional.

**Try this if:** You want something adventurous with Malbec's weight but its own distinct personality. Especially at home with grilled meat.

---

## Primitivo

[INSERT CARD: Primitivo]

**What you'll recognize from Malbec:** Almost everything. Same bold, fruit-forward style, similar tannin, similar warmth. This is the closest thing to a direct swap on the list.

**What's different:** Primitivo is genetically identical to Zinfandel and comes from Puglia in southern Italy. Where Malbec runs toward blueberry and chocolate, Primitivo leans into dried fig, blackberry jam, and black pepper. Higher alcohol, richer finish. Italian character where Malbec has Argentine character.

**Try this if:** You want something extremely close to Malbec in weight and style but with a different address on the label. It's also often the best value here. Solid bottles under $15 are common.

## Food pairings that work across the group

Malbec's profile, dark fruit, medium-high tannin, and lower acidity, makes it a natural match with grilled and roasted red meat. The tannin grips fat, the fruit plays off charred edges. That logic transfers to every wine on this list.

Where Grenache and Nero d'Avola offer more acidity, you get more flexibility at the table. They'll hold up alongside pasta and lighter dishes where a full Malbec might overpower.

Pairings that work for all six:
- Steak, grilled, roasted, or braised
- BBQ, ribs, brisket, pulled pork
- Lamb chops or leg of lamb
- Empanadas, especially beef
- Hard aged cheese: manchego, aged cheddar, pecorino
- Mushroom-heavy dishes

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q. What wine is similar to Malbec but lighter?**
A. Grenache. It keeps the fruit-forward, low-acidity profile that makes Malbec easy to drink, but with less tannin and a bit more lift. Spanish Garnacha is the same grape, often cheaper, and works everywhere Malbec does.

**Q. Is Malbec similar to Cabernet Sauvignon?**
A. They're in the same family, both bold reds with dark fruit and tannin, but Cabernet is significantly firmer and more structured. Malbec is smoother and more immediately drinkable. If you find Cab too grippy, Malbec is the friendlier version. They're also related: Malbec is one of the six grapes permitted in Bordeaux blends alongside Cabernet.

**Q. What's the difference between Malbec and Syrah?**
A. Malbec is fruitier and smoother. Syrah is spicy and more complex. Both are bold and dark-fruited, but Syrah adds black pepper as a defining note that Malbec doesn't have. If you've been drinking Malbec for a while and want something that rewards more attention, Syrah is the natural next step.

**Q. Are there good Malbec alternatives under $15?**
A. Yes. Primitivo from Puglia is genetically the same grape as Zinfandel and drinks like a richer Malbec cousin. Solid bottles regularly come in under $15. Nero d'Avola from Sicily is another strong dark-fruit pick in that range.

**Q. Can you drink Malbec with fish?**
A. You can, but it's not the best match. The tannin and dark fruit tend to overwhelm delicate seafood. If you want a red with fish, Grenache handles it more cleanly. More acidity, less tannin, less likely to fight the food.
